\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We develop a deterministic compartmental ordinary differential equation model for the lifecycle of rage-bait content on social media. The model tracks four content states (ignored, viral, fatigued, and suppressed) together with a latent discussion-pressure variable $\tau$ and an active user pool subject to pressure-dependent attrition. Throughout this report, $\tau$ denotes reply-driven, high-arousal discussion pressure rather than a direct text-classifier score. We calibrate the spread block on the SNAP Higgs Twitter retweet stream, use same-dataset reply activity as the observable proxy for this pressure state, and compare its scale against an external Jigsaw text-toxicity reference from a different platform domain. For the calibrated parameter regime studied here, the fitted model produces a clear amplification threshold at $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$, below which viral spread is not self-sustaining. Above this threshold, stronger amplification raises long-run viral volume and discussion pressure while lowering user retention. A core feature of the model is the pressure feedback loop: viral content drives reply activity and latent pressure, which amplifies further spread and accelerates user departure. Our main contribution is the content-centric framing: unlike most existing compartmental models for social-media dynamics, which classify users into behavioural states, we classify content items into lifecycle compartments and couple them to a pressure state and user attrition. The principal limitation is that the pressure block is proxy-grounded rather than directly calibrated to same-platform text toxicity, so the pressure parameters are only partially identified from the available data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Algorithmic amplification and platform health}

Social media platforms use recommendation algorithms to select and rank content for users. These algorithms are typically optimized for engagement metrics such as clicks, shares, and time-on-platform. Recent work shows that engagement-optimized algorithms tend to amplify politically divisive, morally charged, and emotionally provocative content \cite{huszar2022, milli2025, brady2017}. Husz\'{a}r et al.\ \cite{huszar2022} conducted a large-scale audit of Twitter's algorithmic timeline and found that the algorithm amplified content from political parties, with particularly strong effects for politically right-leaning sources in several countries. Milli et al.\ \cite{milli2025} showed that content promoted by engagement-based algorithms tends to be more divisive and less satisfying to users than content they would have chosen themselves. Brady et al.\ \cite{brady2017} demonstrated that moral-emotional language in social media posts significantly increases their diffusion, with each additional moral-emotional word raising the retweet rate by roughly 20\%.

This suggests a tradeoff at the platform level. Amplification can increase short-run engagement, but it may simultaneously intensify harmful discussion dynamics, increase user fatigue, and erode long-run platform health. Beknazar-Yuzbashev et al.\ \cite{beknazar2025} give experimental evidence for this: a field experiment showed that exposure to toxic content on social media reduced subsequent user engagement, suggesting that toxicity imposes real costs on platform participation.

\subsection{Compartmental models for information spread}

Compartmental models from epidemiology have been applied widely to information dynamics. The classical SIR (Susceptible-Infected-Recovered) model partitions a population into compartments and describes transitions between them using ordinary differential equations. Daley and Kendall \cite{daleyKendall1964} noted the analogy between disease transmission and information spread early on and proposed a rumour-spreading model with similar compartmental structure. In the social media setting, De Domenico et al.\ \cite{domenico2013} used the Higgs Twitter dataset to study the anatomy of a scientific rumour, fitting epidemic-style models to retweet cascades.

The DABAR modelling procedure taught in this course (\emph{Define, Assume, Build, Assess, Report}) is the framework we follow. We define the modelling question around platform amplification tradeoffs, state our assumptions about aggregate content dynamics, build a six-state compartmental ODE system, assess it against empirical data and analytical checks, and report the results with clear attention to limitations.

The IVFS model we develop falls into the category of a \emph{deterministic dynamical model} in the language of the course. It is a system of first-order autonomous ODEs whose state variables are continuous and whose parameters are either calibrated from data or fixed at scenario values. The model is not stochastic, not optimization-based, and not probabilistic. Its core analytical tools are equilibrium analysis, linearized stability, threshold quantities, and sensitivity indices, all of which are covered extensively in the course material.

\subsection{The content-centric gap}

Most existing compartmental models for social-media dynamics classify \emph{users} into behavioural states. For example, an SVFR-type model might place users into susceptible, viewing, forwarding, and recovered compartments \cite{liShao2024, jing2018}. Toxicity-focused models similarly track user infection states, where ``infection'' represents adoption of toxic behaviour \cite{maleki2022, addai2025}. These models are valuable, but they require assumptions about individual user decision-making and local network structure that are difficult to validate at platform scale.

Our model takes a different approach. Instead of compartmentalizing users, we compartmentalize \emph{content items} into lifecycle states: content is created, may go viral, eventually fatigues, and may be moderated. This content-centric framing works at the aggregate level where platform data is most available (retweet counts, reply volumes, and moderation actions are all content-level observables), separates amplification (which acts on content visibility) from user response (captured by a single aggregate user pool), and lets us study the platform health tradeoff directly through content lifecycle dynamics rather than individual user psychology.

The tradeoff is that we lose resolution on user heterogeneity and network topology. We treat users as a single aggregate pool $U$ with constant replenishment and pressure-dependent attrition. This keeps the system tractable (six states rather than dozens) while still capturing the feedback loop between content amplification, discussion pressure, and platform health.

\subsection{The dataset at a glance}

We use the SNAP Higgs Twitter dataset \cite{domenico2013} as our main data source: a record of approximately 563{,}000 social interactions captured during a 168-hour observation window centred on the July 2012 Higgs boson announcement. The dataset records three event types, namely retweets (RT), replies (RE), and mentions (MT), with Unix-epoch timestamps at one-second resolution. Figure~\ref{fig:higgs_overview} shows the hourly activity for each type. The dominant feature is a sharp retweet cascade in the first 24 hours, followed by a slower decay. Reply activity, which we later use as a proxy for discussion pressure, tracks the retweet peak with a slight lag. Mention activity is noisier and lower in volume.

\subsection{Operational definition of rage-bait}
\label{sec:ragebait-def}

We define \emph{rage-bait content} operationally as content that generates disproportionate discussion activity relative to its passive resharing volume. In the model, this idea is represented by a high pressure-to-virality ratio $\tau/V$ when $V>0$. This is a model-internal structural definition, not an independently validated external content label: content is ``rage-bait'' here whenever the pressure-to-virality ratio is elevated, regardless of the topic's political valence or emotional register. During the main Higgs cascade, reply and mention activity surge well beyond what the retweet volume alone would predict (Figure~\ref{fig:higgs_overview}b and c), consistent with a high-discussion event in which the announcement provokes intense conversation rather than only passive resharing. We therefore use the Higgs cascade as a high-discussion demonstration case for the IVFS dynamics, even though the underlying content is scientific rather than politically provocative.

Throughout the report, the state $\tau$ should be read primarily as reply-driven discussion pressure. When we occasionally use the word \emph{toxicity}, we mean this same proxy-grounded pressure quantity rather than a direct text label. When we specifically mean \emph{Jigsaw toxicity}, we mean the external text-level annotation scores from the Jigsaw dataset, which are used only as an outside reference for scale.

Throughout this report we return to one central question: how does algorithmic amplification change the long-run balance between virality, discussion pressure, and user retention in a content-centric social-media system? The sections that follow build the model, establish the threshold, calibrate it against data, and trace the answer through to a quantitative policy comparison.

\section{Previous mathematical models}
\label{sec:previous}

We review the most relevant prior models and where our IVFS model differs.

\subsection{Rumour models and epidemic analogies}

The idea that information spreads like a disease dates to Daley and Kendall \cite{daleyKendall1964}, who proposed a stochastic model for rumour transmission in which ``spreaders'' contact ``ignorants'' and either transmit the rumour or become ``stiflers.'' This model has a natural compartmental structure analogous to SIR, and its threshold behaviour (rumours persist only when the contact rate exceeds a critical value) mirrors the basic reproduction number $\mathcal{R}_0$ in epidemiology.

De Domenico et al.\ \cite{domenico2013} applied epidemic-style modelling to the same Higgs Twitter dataset that we use. They studied the temporal evolution of the retweet cascade around the Higgs boson discovery announcement and fitted several spreading models to the data. Their work demonstrates that compartmental models can capture the burst-and-decay dynamics of real social media cascades, which is one of the empirical motivations for our project.

\subsection{User-centric compartmental models}

Several recent papers have developed compartmental ODE models specifically for social media dynamics, but with \emph{users} as the compartmentalized entities.

Li and Shao \cite{liShao2024} proposed an SVFR (Susceptible-Viewing-Forwarding-Recovered) model for viral video sharing. In their framework, users transition from viewing to forwarding based on content attractiveness, and advertising interventions can modify transition rates. The model is fitted to video-sharing data and produces threshold conditions for viral spread. Jing et al.\ \cite{jing2018} developed a similar SIR-based model for advertising spread on social networks, incorporating network structure through degree distributions.

These models give useful threshold results, but they assume user-level states that are hard to observe at platform scale. For example, classifying a user as ``susceptible'' or ``recovered'' requires knowledge of their exposure history, which is typically not available in public datasets.

\subsection{Toxicity propagation models}

A separate line of work models the propagation of toxic or harmful content on social media using epidemic-style compartmental structures.

Maleki et al.\ \cite{maleki2022} applied an epidemiological model to evaluate toxicity propagation on Twitter. Their model classifies users into compartments based on their toxicity exposure and production, and they calibrate it using text-based toxicity scores from Twitter data. Addai et al.\ \cite{addai2025} extended this approach by modelling interventions (content moderation, counter-speech, and user education) as control variables in a toxicity-propagation ODE system and studying their effects through simulation.

Both of these models use direct text-based toxicity labels, which is an advantage over our proxy-based approach. However, both also compartmentalize \emph{users} rather than content, and neither explicitly models the feedback loop between content amplification, discussion pressure, and user retention that is central to our model.

\section{Modelling question and approach}
\label{sec:question}

\subsection{Modelling question}

Our main question is:
\begin{quote}
\emph{How does platform amplification shape the full lifecycle of rage-bait content, from creation through virality, fatigue, and suppression, and what are the downstream consequences for reply-driven discussion pressure and long-run user retention?}
\end{quote}

We investigate five subsidiary questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item At what amplification level does viral spread become self-sustaining, and what is the mathematical nature of this threshold (transcritical bifurcation, forward or backward)?
    \item How does the pressure feedback loop (where viral content drives reply activity and latent discussion pressure, which in turn amplifies further spread) affect the equilibrium structure of the system?
    \item How sensitive are the long-run equilibrium outcomes ($V^*$, $\tau^*$, $U^*$) to the parameters governing the pressure block, and which parameters matter most?
    \item Can a single dataset (the SNAP Higgs Twitter activity stream) support both a spread calibration and a pressure proxy, using reply activity as the observable signal of heightened discussion pressure?
    \item What is the quantitative tradeoff between platform engagement and platform health, and is there an amplification level that balances the two?
\end{enumerate}

These questions fit a compartmental ODE framework: they involve thresholds, equilibria, bifurcation, and sensitivity, all standard tools from the course. The lifecycle framing means we track content through its full trajectory on the platform, not just the initial viral spike.


\subsection{Modelling approach}
\label{sec:approach}

We use a deterministic six-state compartmental ordinary differential equation (ODE) system. In the course's classification, this is a \emph{deterministic dynamical model}: the state evolves continuously in time according to a system of first-order ODEs, and there is no stochastic or probabilistic component.

The modelling approach follows the DABAR procedure:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Define.} We define the modelling question around the amplification and health tradeoff, with content lifecycle states as the primary dynamical variables.
    \item \textbf{Assume.} We assume that aggregate content and user activity can be approximated by continuous deterministic flows, that content items can be grouped into four lifecycle compartments, and that harmful discussion pressure can be summarized by a single latent scalar state.
    \item \textbf{Build.} We construct a six-state ODE system with content compartments $(I,V,F,S)$, a pressure state $\tau$, and a user pool $U$, with couplings that capture the amplification feedback loop.
    \item \textbf{Assess.} We calibrate the spread block against the Higgs Twitter dataset, benchmark against a classical SIR alternative, validate on multiple time windows, and perform sensitivity and robustness analyses.
    \item \textbf{Report.} We present the results with clear attention to what is well-identified (spread parameters) and what is only partially identified (pressure parameters).
\end{enumerate}

The empirical strategy has two stages. First, we calibrate the spread block using a reduced IVF helper model fitted to the Higgs retweet stream. This stage identifies the spread-side parameters $(\beta_0, \gamma_0, \lambda_0, d_\lambda)$ with narrow confidence intervals. Second, we simulate the full six-state IVFS model under different amplification scenarios to study the policy tradeoff. The pressure block is handled separately: same-dataset reply activity (RE) serves as a proxy for latent discussion pressure, and the Jigsaw toxicity dataset provides an external reference scale for the magnitude of $\tau$, but neither source uniquely pins down the pressure parameters $(\phi, \psi)$.

This split means the spread calibration is clean and well-identified, while the pressure block is reported as partially constrained. This is better than forcing a joint calibration that would mix well-identified and poorly-identified parameters.

\section{Formulation of the model}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{State variables}
\label{sec:states}

The IVFS model is a deterministic compartmental ODE system with six state variables. Four are content lifecycle compartments, one captures latent discussion pressure, and one tracks the aggregate active-user pool.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{1.6cm} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{11cm}}
\toprule
Variable & Interpretation \\
\midrule
$I(t)$ & Ignored or not-yet-viral content. This compartment represents content items that have been posted on the platform but have not yet achieved significant visibility. Content enters the system through $I$. \\[0.3em]
$V(t)$ & Viral content. Content items that have achieved high visibility and are being actively shared and engaged with. This is the compartment most directly affected by the amplification parameter $\alpha$. \\[0.3em]
$F(t)$ & Fatigued content. Content that was previously viral but has lost engagement momentum. Users have moved on, and the content is no longer being actively shared. \\[0.3em]
$S(t)$ & Suppressed or moderated content. Content that has been removed or de-ranked by platform moderation. In the current model, moderation acts only on ignored content ($I \to S$). \\[0.3em]
$\tau(t)$ & Latent reply-driven discussion pressure. A scalar state variable representing the aggregate level of high-arousal discussion pressure on the platform. In the calibration, $\tau$ is proxied by same-dataset reply volume (RE events from Higgs), which is the behavioural signal most directly consistent with heated discussion dynamics: content that provokes replies rather than passive reshares. This is not directly observed text toxicity; it is a latent variable driven by viral content and relaxing over time. \\[0.3em]
$U(t)$ & Active users. The aggregate number of users actively participating on the platform. This pool is replenished at a constant rate and depleted at a rate that increases with discussion pressure. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The four content compartments $(I, V, F, S)$ form a lifecycle: content is created (enters $I$), may go viral (moves to $V$), eventually fatigues (moves to $F$), and may be moderated at any point (moves to $S$). All content also decays naturally at rate $\mu_c$, representing loss of visibility over time. This lifecycle structure is the core of the model and is what distinguishes it from user-centric approaches.

The amplification parameter $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is the key policy variable. It represents the degree to which the platform's recommendation algorithm promotes engaging content. Setting $\alpha$ close to 1 corresponds to aggressive engagement optimization; setting $\alpha$ close to 0 corresponds to a health-first policy that limits algorithmic amplification.

\subsection{Modelling assumptions}
\label{sec:assumptions}

The assumptions in the IVFS model are not arbitrary simplifications added for convenience. Each one is a modelling choice tied to the level of data available and to the specific policy question we want to answer. The Higgs dataset gives aggregate event streams, not individual exposure histories, so the right standard is whether an assumption is appropriate for aggregate platform scale dynamics. With that in mind, the model uses the following assumptions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Continuous deterministic flows.} Aggregate content and user activity can be approximated by continuous variables evolving according to deterministic ODEs. This is appropriate here because the calibration window contains very large event counts, so the main object of interest is the mean cascade shape rather than individual retweet randomness. The deterministic approximation is therefore a deliberate coarse graining step, not an attempt to model micro-level behaviour.

    \item \textbf{Content lifecycle compartments.} Content items can be meaningfully grouped into four lifecycle stages: ignored, viral, fatigued, and suppressed. These boundaries are stylized, but they are exactly the level at which platform policy acts. Platforms tune visibility, moderation, and ranking at the content level, so a content-centric partition is the right abstraction for studying amplification policy.

    \item \textbf{Homogeneous mixing.} We assume that all content items in a given compartment have equal probability of transitioning to other compartments. This loses network topology and community structure, but it is what allows the model to answer a platform-level control question with public aggregate data. A network-aware model would require information that the present dataset does not contain.

    \item \textbf{Scalar pressure.} Harmful discussion dynamics are summarized by a single scalar state $\tau$. This does not claim that all forms of online harm are the same. It claims only that, for the present question, a one-dimensional reply-driven discussion-pressure variable is enough to capture the feedback loop between virality, heated reply activity, and user loss.

    \item \textbf{Aggregate user pool.} The active-user population is a single aggregate pool with constant replenishment and pressure-dependent attrition. This is justified because the report asks how platform health responds in the aggregate. The variable $U$ is not intended to resolve user types; it is intended to detect the directional retention cost of amplification.

    \item \textbf{Local parameter constancy.} The spread-side parameters $(\beta_0, \gamma_0)$ are treated as locally constant on the 100-hour calibration window. This is an identification choice: we calibrate the dominant burst and immediate decay on a fixed event window, then check robustness by refitting alternate windows and coarser binning choices rather than pretending the same coefficients must hold identically over the full week.
\end{enumerate}

The common theme is that each assumption trades granularity for identifiability. What we learn from this section is that the model is deliberately matched to the resolution of the available data and to the platform-level policy question, even though that leaves important micro-level effects for future work.

\subsection{The IVFS equations}
\label{sec:equations}

The model is the following system of six coupled first-order ODEs:
\begin{align}
\dot{I} &= \underbrace{\frac{\rho U}{1+U}}_{\text{content creation}} - \underbrace{\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\, I\, V}_{\text{viral conversion}} - \underbrace{(\delta + \mu_c)\, I}_{\text{moderation + decay}}, \label{eq:I} \\[6pt]
\dot{V} &= \underbrace{\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\, I\, V}_{\text{viral conversion}} - \underbrace{\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\, V}_{\text{fatigue}} - \underbrace{\mu_c\, V}_{\text{decay}}, \label{eq:V} \\[6pt]
\dot{F} &= \underbrace{\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\, V}_{\text{fatigue inflow}} - \underbrace{\mu_c\, F}_{\text{decay}}, \label{eq:F} \\[6pt]
\dot{S} &= \underbrace{\delta\, I}_{\text{moderation}} - \underbrace{\mu_c\, S}_{\text{decay}}, \label{eq:S} \\[6pt]
\dot{\tau} &= \underbrace{\phi\, V}_{\text{pressure forcing}} - \underbrace{\psi\, \tau}_{\text{pressure relaxation}}, \label{eq:tau} \\[6pt]
\dot{U} &= \underbrace{\nu}_{\text{replenishment}} - \underbrace{\lambda_u (1 + w\tau)\, U}_{\text{pressure-dependent attrition}}, \label{eq:U}
\end{align}
where the effective rates are
\begin{equation}
\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} = \alpha\, \beta_0\, (1 + \kappa\, \tau), \qquad
\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} = \gamma_0\, (1 + \eta\, \tau).
\label{eq:eff_rates}
\end{equation}

Each equation works as follows.

\paragraph{Content creation (Eq.~\ref{eq:I}).} New content enters the ignored compartment at rate $\rho U / (1+U)$. This is a saturating (Michaelis-Menten type) inflow: when the user pool $U$ is small, content creation scales roughly linearly with $U$; when $U$ is large, creation saturates toward the maximum rate $\rho$. A purely linear inflow $\rho U$ made the content input unrealistically large in our simulations, which is why we adopted the saturating form. Content leaves the ignored compartment either by going viral (at rate $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} I V$, which is proportional to the product of ignored and viral content, analogous to the mass-action transmission term in SIR models) or by being moderated ($\delta I$) or decaying naturally ($\mu_c I$).

\paragraph{Viral dynamics (Eq.~\ref{eq:V}).} Content enters the viral compartment through the mass-action conversion term $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} I V$. The effective spread rate $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} = \alpha \beta_0 (1+\kappa\tau)$ captures two effects: the platform amplification parameter $\alpha$ scales the base rate $\beta_0$, and the pressure feedback $(1+\kappa\tau)$ means that higher discussion pressure increases the rate at which content goes viral. This is the amplification feedback loop. Viral content leaves $V$ by fatiguing at rate $\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} V$ or decaying at rate $\mu_c V$.

\paragraph{Fatigue and suppression (Eqs.~\ref{eq:F} and \ref{eq:S}).} These are accumulation compartments. Fatigued content collects previously viral items that have lost momentum; suppressed content collects items removed by moderation. Neither $F$ nor $S$ feeds back into the spread-pressure-user dynamics. They are ``sinks'' in the feedback structure, but they are important for interpreting the content lifecycle and for total content accounting.

\paragraph{Discussion pressure (Eq.~\ref{eq:tau}).} The latent pressure state $\tau$ is driven by viral content at rate $\phi V$ and relaxes at rate $\psi\tau$. This is a simple first-order linear response: pressure builds when viral content is present and decays exponentially when it is not. The equilibrium pressure level (when $V$ is constant) is $\tau^* = \phi V^* / \psi$, so the ratio $\phi/\psi$ sets the scale of long-run pressure.

\paragraph{User dynamics (Eq.~\ref{eq:U}).} Users join the platform at a constant rate $\nu$ and leave at rate $\lambda_u(1+w\tau)U$. The important part is pressure-dependent attrition: higher discussion pressure causes faster user departure. At equilibrium, $U^* = \nu / [\lambda_u(1+w\tau^*)]$, so increased pressure directly reduces the steady-state user base.

\paragraph{Effective rates (Eq.~\ref{eq:eff_rates}).} The effective spread rate $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}$ and effective fatigue rate $\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}$ both depend on the pressure $\tau$. Higher pressure increases both rates: it makes content spread faster (viral amplification) but also fatigue faster (burnout effect). This creates a nonlinear feedback: amplification drives viral content, which drives pressure, which in turn modifies both the amplification and fatigue rates.

\subsection{Structure of the feedback loop}

Figure~\ref{fig:structure} shows the state-flow structure of the model. The essential feedback loop is:
\[
V \xrightarrow{\phi} \tau \xrightarrow{\kappa} \beta_{\mathrm{eff}} \xrightarrow{} V.
\]
Viral content drives pressure, pressure amplifies the effective spread rate, and the amplified spread rate produces more viral content. This positive feedback loop is counteracted by fatigue ($V \to F$), pressure relaxation ($\psi\tau$), and pressure-dependent user attrition ($\lambda_u w\tau U$), which reduces the content creation rate.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering

\subsection{Threshold quantity and disease-free equilibrium}
\label{sec:threshold}

A central quantity in any compartmental model is the threshold that determines whether the ``infection'' (in our case, viral content) can persist in the long run. Following standard practice in epidemic modelling, we compute this threshold by linearizing around the disease-free equilibrium (DFE).

At the DFE, there is no viral content: $V = 0$, $\tau = 0$, $F = 0$. The remaining equations give:
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{DFE}} = \frac{\nu}{\lambda_u}, \qquad
I_{\mathrm{DFE}} = \frac{\rho\, U_{\mathrm{DFE}}}{(1 + U_{\mathrm{DFE}})(\delta + \mu_c)}.
\label{eq:dfe}
\end{equation}
At the DFE, the suppressed-content compartment satisfies $\dot{S} = \delta I - \mu_c S = 0$, giving
\[
S_{\mathrm{DFE}} = \frac{\delta\, I_{\mathrm{DFE}}}{\mu_c}.
\]
With the current parameter values, $U_{\mathrm{DFE}} = 50.0$, $I_{\mathrm{DFE}} \approx 0.9804$, and $S_{\mathrm{DFE}} = 0.05 \times 0.9804 / 0.01 \approx 4.902$.

Linearizing the $\dot{V}$ equation around the DFE yields
\[
\dot{V} \approx \bigl(\alpha\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}} - \gamma_0 - \mu_c\bigr) V.
\]
Viral content grows when the coefficient is positive and decays when it is negative. We define the basic reproduction number:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\mathcal{R}_0 = \frac{\alpha\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}}{\gamma_0 + \mu_c}.}
\label{eq:R0}
\end{equation}
Viral content is self-sustaining when $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ and dies out when $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$. Solving $\mathcal{R}_0 = 1$ for $\alpha$ gives the critical amplification level:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} = \frac{\gamma_0 + \mu_c}{\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}} \approx 0.3165.
\label{eq:alpha_crit}
\end{equation}

This threshold divides the amplification parameter space into two regimes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item $\alpha < \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$: the DFE is stable, viral content dies out, and $\tau \to 0$.
    \item $\alpha > \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$: the DFE is unstable. For the calibrated parameter set analyzed below, a unique positive equilibrium with $V^* > 0$ and $\tau^* > 0$ also exists.
\end{itemize}

This $\mathcal{R}_0$ comes from linearizing the $\dot{V}$ equation at the DFE. A full next-generation matrix derivation would strengthen this result; we discuss this as a future extension in Section~\ref{sec:nextsteps}.

The mechanism the model proposes is a nonlinear amplification-pressure feedback loop: platform amplification $\alpha$ converts ignored content to viral, viral content builds latent discussion pressure, and that pressure boosts further spread while eroding the user base. Whether this feedback self-sustains depends entirely on whether $\alpha$ exceeds $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$.

In the numerical implementation, the full IVFS trajectories and policy sweeps are integrated with SciPy's adaptive `solve\_ivp` solver using explicit tolerances and a one-hour maximum step. The reduced three-state Higgs helper fit is kept on the older `odeint` routine so that the calibration reproduces the reported spread estimates exactly. The code also clamps small negative state excursions back to zero inside the right-hand side evaluation for the full six-state system. This does not change the analytical model; it is a numerical safeguard that keeps the computed trajectory inside the nonnegative region established above.

\section{Analysis of the model}
\label{sec:analysis}

This section presents the analytical results we have obtained for the IVFS system. We establish nonnegativity, boundedness, the DFE threshold, local stability, and sensitivity indices.

\subsection{Nonnegativity}

\begin{proposition}[Nonnegativity]
\label{prop:nonneg}
If the initial state satisfies $I(0), V(0), F(0), S(0), \tau(0), U(0) \geq 0$, then the solution remains componentwise nonnegative for as long as it exists.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We verify that each coordinate hyperplane is inward-pointing or tangent.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item At $I = 0$: $\dot{I} = \rho U/(1+U) \geq 0$, since $U \geq 0$.
    \item At $V = 0$: $\dot{V} = 0$, since both the conversion and fatigue terms vanish.
    \item At $F = 0$: $\dot{F} = \gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} V \geq 0$, since $V \geq 0$ and $\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} > 0$.
    \item At $S = 0$: $\dot{S} = \delta I \geq 0$, since $I \geq 0$ and $\delta > 0$.
    \item At $\tau = 0$: $\dot{\tau} = \phi V \geq 0$, since $V \geq 0$ and $\phi > 0$.
    \item At $U = 0$: $\dot{U} = \nu > 0$, since $\nu > 0$.
\end{itemize}
Since the vector field points inward (or is tangent) on every face of the nonnegative orthant $\mathbb{R}^6_{\geq 0}$, the orthant is positively invariant.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundedness}

\begin{proposition}[Boundedness]
\label{prop:bounded}
For every nonnegative initial condition, the corresponding solution remains in a compact positively invariant region. In particular, every state variable stays bounded for all forward time.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We bound each group of variables in turn.

\emph{User pool.} From \eqref{eq:U}, $\dot{U} = \nu - \lambda_u(1+w\tau)U \leq \nu - \lambda_u U$, since $\tau \geq 0$. By the comparison lemma,
\[
U(t) \leq \max\!\left\{U(0), \frac{\nu}{\lambda_u}\right\}
\qquad \text{for all } t \geq 0.
\]
Set $U_{\max} = \max\{U(0), \nu/\lambda_u\}$.

\emph{Total content mass.} Define $C = I + V + F + S$. Summing equations \eqref{eq:I} to \eqref{eq:S}, the internal transfer terms ($\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} IV$, $\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} V$, $\delta I$) cancel, leaving:
\[
\dot{C} = \frac{\rho U}{1+U} - \mu_c C \leq \rho - \mu_c C.
\]
Hence
\[
C(t) \leq \max\!\left\{C(0), \frac{\rho}{\mu_c}\right\}
\qquad \text{for all } t \geq 0.
\]
Set $C_{\max} = \max\{C(0), \rho/\mu_c\}$.

\emph{Pressure.} Since $V \leq C \leq C_{\max}$, equation \eqref{eq:tau} gives $\dot{\tau} \leq \phi C_{\max} - \psi\tau$, so
\[
\tau(t) \leq \max\!\left\{\tau(0), \frac{\phi C_{\max}}{\psi}\right\}
\qquad \text{for all } t \geq 0.
\]
Set $\tau_{\max} = \max\{\tau(0), \phi C_{\max}/\psi\}$.

Because $I,V,F,S \geq 0$ and $I+V+F+S=C \leq C_{\max}$, each of the four content compartments is individually bounded by $C_{\max}$. Therefore the trajectory remains in the compact box
\[
[0,C_{\max}]^4 \times [0,\tau_{\max}] \times [0,U_{\max}],
\]
which is positively invariant for this initial condition.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Propositions~\ref{prop:nonneg} and \ref{prop:bounded} together guarantee that the IVFS system is well-posed: solutions exist for all forward time, remain nonneg\-ative, and stay bounded. This lets us do the equilibrium and stability analysis that follows.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Disease-free equilibrium and threshold}

\begin{proposition}[DFE stability threshold]
\label{prop:dfe}
The disease-free equilibrium $(I_{\mathrm{DFE}}, 0, 0, S_{\mathrm{DFE}}, 0, U_{\mathrm{DFE}})$ is locally asymptotically stable when $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$ and unstable when $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$, where $\mathcal{R}_0$ is given by \eqref{eq:R0}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At the DFE, $V = \tau = 0$. If we reorder the state variables as $(V,\tau,U,I,S,F)$, the Jacobian becomes lower triangular, so its eigenvalues are its diagonal entries. The eigenvalue governing the $V$ direction is
\[
\mu_V = \alpha\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}} - \gamma_0 - \mu_c = (\gamma_0 + \mu_c)(\mathcal{R}_0 - 1).
\]
When $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$, this eigenvalue is negative. The remaining five eigenvalues of the block-triangular Jacobian at the DFE are:
\[
\mu_\tau = -\psi, \quad
\mu_F = -\mu_c, \quad
\mu_I = -(\delta + \mu_c), \quad
\mu_S = -\mu_c, \quad
\mu_U = -\lambda_u,
\]
all of which are strictly negative since every parameter is positive. Hence the DFE is locally asymptotically stable. When $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$, the $V$ eigenvalue is positive and the DFE is unstable.
\end{proof}

For the calibrated parameter set used in this report, we can also determine the direction of the local bifurcation at $\alpha = \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ using the center-manifold criterion of Castillo-Chavez and Song~\cite{castilloChavez2004}.

\begin{proposition}[Forward transcritical bifurcation]
\label{prop:forward}
For the calibrated parameter values in Table~\ref{tab:params}, the transcritical bifurcation at $\alpha = \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ is forward: the positive equilibrium branch emerging from the DFE for $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ has $V^* > 0$ and is locally asymptotically stable near the bifurcation point.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Proposition~\ref{prop:dfe} establishes that the Jacobian $J$ at the DFE has a simple zero eigenvalue when $\mathcal{R}_0 = 1$, with all five remaining eigenvalues strictly negative. We compute the two scalar coefficients $a$ and $b$ that determine the bifurcation direction.

\emph{Left eigenvector.}
At the DFE with $V = \tau = 0$, every entry in the $V$-row of $J$ either contains a factor of $V$ or evaluates to $\mu_V = 0$. Hence the $V$-row is identically zero, and the left eigenvector is $v = e_V$ (i.e.\ $v_V = 1$, all other components zero).

\emph{Right eigenvector.}
Let $q$ denote the right eigenvector and normalize it by $q_V = 1$, so that $v \cdot q = 1$. Solving $Jq = 0$, the $\tau$-row gives $\phi - \psi q_\tau = 0$, i.e.\ $q_\tau = \phi/\psi$. The $U$-row gives $q_U = -w\,U_{\mathrm{DFE}}\,\phi/\psi$. The $F$-row gives $q_F = \gamma_0/\mu_c$, and the $S$-row gives $q_S = \delta q_I / \mu_c$. The $I$-row gives:
\begin{equation}
q_I = -\frac{\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}} + \frac{\rho\,w\,U_{\mathrm{DFE}}\,\phi}{\psi\,(1+U_{\mathrm{DFE}})^2}}{\delta + \mu_c}\;.
\label{eq:qI}
\end{equation}
Since every quantity in the numerator is positive, $q_I < 0$.

\emph{Coefficient~$a$.}
Since $v = e_V$, only second partial derivatives of $f_V = \alpha\beta_0(1{+}\kappa\tau)\,I\,V - \gamma_0(1{+}\eta\tau)\,V - \mu_c V$ contribute. At the DFE, the only nonzero second partials are
\[
\frac{\partial^2 f_V}{\partial I\,\partial V} = \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}\,\beta_0, \qquad
\frac{\partial^2 f_V}{\partial \tau\,\partial V} = \kappa(\gamma_0 {+} \mu_c) - \gamma_0\,\eta,
\]
where the second uses $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}\beta_0 I_{\mathrm{DFE}} = \gamma_0 + \mu_c$. Both are symmetric, so
\[
 a \;=\; 2\,q_I\,\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}\,\beta_0 \;+\; 2\,\frac{\phi}{\psi}\bigl[\kappa(\gamma_0 {+} \mu_c) - \gamma_0\,\eta\bigr].
\]
The first term is strictly negative ($q_I < 0$). The second term is positive under the calibrated parameters ($\kappa(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c) = 0.248 > \gamma_0\eta = 0.090$), but its magnitude is small: numerically, $a \approx -3.34 + 0.18 = -3.16 < 0$.

\emph{Coefficient~$b$.}
The only nonzero mixed partial $\partial^2 f_V/\partial x_i\,\partial\alpha$ at the DFE is $\partial^2 f_V/\partial V\,\partial\alpha = \beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$, so
\[
b = \beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}} \approx 0.98 > 0.
\]

Since $a < 0$ and $b > 0$, the criterion of \cite{castilloChavez2004} guarantees a forward transcritical bifurcation: the positive equilibrium exists for $\alpha$ slightly above $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ and is locally asymptotically stable near the bifurcation point.
\end{proof}

Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation} illustrates this result numerically. Panel~(a) plots $\mathcal{R}_0$ as a function of $\alpha$, with the threshold at $\mathcal{R}_0 = 1$ clearly marked. Panel~(b) shows the equilibrium viral volume $V^*$ obtained from the positive-equilibrium calculation for each value of~$\alpha$. The sharp onset at $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$ and the smooth forward growth of $V^*$ are consistent with the forward transcritical bifurcation established in Proposition~\ref{prop:forward}.

\subsection{Positive equilibrium}

For the calibrated parameter set, we can solve for the positive equilibrium when $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ by setting all time derivatives to zero. The pressure equation \eqref{eq:tau} gives $\tau^* = \phi V^* / \psi$, and the user equation \eqref{eq:U} gives $U^* = \nu / [\lambda_u(1 + w\tau^*)]$. The viral equation \eqref{eq:V} with $V^* > 0$ gives $I^* = (\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} + \mu_c)/\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}$. Substituting into the ignored-content equation \eqref{eq:I} reduces the system to a single scalar equation.

\begin{proposition}[Existence and uniqueness of the positive equilibrium]
\label{prop:unique}
For the calibrated parameter values in Table~\ref{tab:params}, whenever $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ there exists a unique positive equilibrium with all six components strictly positive.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\sigma = \phi V^*/\psi \geq 0$, so that $\tau^* = \sigma$ and $V^* = \psi\sigma/\phi$. The equilibrium relations give
\[
U^*(\sigma) = \frac{\nu}{\lambda_u(1+w\sigma)}, \qquad
I^*(\sigma) = \frac{\gamma_0(1+\eta\sigma)+\mu_c}{\alpha\beta_0(1+\kappa\sigma)}\;.
\]
Substituting into the $\dot{I} = 0$ equation (using $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} I^* V^* = (\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}+\mu_c)V^*$) gives the scalar equation $g(\sigma) = 0$, where
\[
g(\sigma) = \underbrace{\frac{\rho\,\nu}{\lambda_u(1{+}w\sigma)+\nu}}_{\displaystyle L(\sigma)} - \underbrace{\left[\frac{[\gamma_0(1{+}\eta\sigma)+\mu_c]\,\psi\sigma}{\phi} + (\delta{+}\mu_c)\,I^*(\sigma)\right]}_{\displaystyle R(\sigma)}.
\]

\emph{Boundary values.}
At $\sigma = 0$: $L(0) = \rho U_{\mathrm{DFE}}/(1{+}U_{\mathrm{DFE}}) = (\delta{+}\mu_c)\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$ (the DFE relation), and $R(0) = (\delta{+}\mu_c)(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c)/(\alpha\beta_0) = (\delta{+}\mu_c)\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}/\mathcal{R}_0$, so
\[
g(0) = (\delta{+}\mu_c)\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{\mathcal{R}_0}\right) > 0 \quad\text{when } \mathcal{R}_0 > 1.
\]
As $\sigma \to \infty$: $L(\sigma) \to 0$ while the first term of $R(\sigma)$ grows at least linearly, so $g(\sigma) \to -\infty$.

\emph{Monotonicity.}
$L(\sigma)$ is strictly decreasing ($L'(\sigma) < 0$).
For $R(\sigma)$, the first term has derivative $\psi(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c)/\phi + 2\psi\gamma_0\eta\sigma/\phi > 0$ for all $\sigma \geq 0$. The second term $(\delta{+}\mu_c)\,I^*(\sigma)$ has derivative
\[
\frac{(\delta{+}\mu_c)\,[\gamma_0\eta - \kappa(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c)]}{\alpha\beta_0\,(1{+}\kappa\sigma)^2}\;,
\]
which is negative (since $\gamma_0\eta = 0.090 < \kappa(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c) = 0.248$ under the calibrated parameters), but bounded in magnitude by $(\delta{+}\mu_c)[\kappa(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c)-\gamma_0\eta]/(\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}\beta_0) \approx 0.030$. The positive contribution from the first term satisfies $\psi(\gamma_0{+}\mu_c)/\phi \approx 0.554 \gg 0.030$, so $R'(\sigma) > 0$ for all $\sigma \geq 0$ and all $\alpha \geq \alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$.

Therefore $g'(\sigma) = L'(\sigma) - R'(\sigma) < 0$ for all $\sigma \geq 0$: the function $g$ is strictly decreasing. Since $g(0) > 0$ and $g(\sigma) \to -\infty$, the intermediate value theorem gives exactly one root $\sigma^* > 0$. The remaining components $V^* = \psi\sigma^*/\phi$, $\tau^* = \sigma^*$, $U^*$, $I^*$, $F^* = \gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} V^*/\mu_c$, and $S^* = \delta I^*/\mu_c$ are all strictly positive.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Numerical local stability checks at the positive equilibrium}

The repository numerically evaluates the Jacobian of the full six-state system at the positive equilibrium for each scenario. The current numerical results are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item At $\alpha = 0.2$ ($\mathcal{R}_0 \approx 0.632$): below threshold, no positive equilibrium exists.
    \item At $\alpha = 0.5$ ($\mathcal{R}_0 \approx 1.580$): all eigenvalues of the Jacobian at the positive equilibrium have negative real part. The equilibrium is locally asymptotically stable.
    \item At $\alpha = 0.9$ ($\mathcal{R}_0 \approx 2.844$): all eigenvalues of the Jacobian at the positive equilibrium have negative real part. The equilibrium is locally asymptotically stable.
\end{itemize}
The leading (least negative) real part of the eigenvalues at the positive equilibrium is approximately $-0.01$, which corresponds to the slow natural content decay eigenvalue from the decoupled sink compartments $F$ and $S$.

A fully symbolic proof of local asymptotic stability of the positive equilibrium is beyond the scope of this report, because the six-dimensional Jacobian does not factor into small enough blocks for clean hand computation. The numerical Jacobian checks are consistent across the calibrated scenarios we tested and support local stability for those cases, but they do not by themselves prove stability for every admissible parameter choice.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

We now check how sensitive the model outputs are to parameter changes. Following the course material, we define the \emph{normalized sensitivity index} of a quantity $x$ with respect to a parameter $p$ as:
\begin{equation}
S(x, p) = \frac{\partial x}{\partial p} \cdot \frac{p}{x}.
\label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}
This measures the proportional change in $x$ per proportional change in $p$.

We compute the sensitivity of $\mathcal{R}_0$ with respect to the key parameters analytically. Since
\[
\mathcal{R}_0 = \frac{\alpha\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}}}{\gamma_0 + \mu_c},
\]
and $I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$ depends on $\rho$, $\delta$, $\mu_c$, $\nu$, and $\lambda_u$ through \eqref{eq:dfe}, we obtain:
\begin{align*}
S(\mathcal{R}_0, \alpha) &= +1, \\
S(\mathcal{R}_0, \beta_0) &= +1, \\
S(\mathcal{R}_0, \gamma_0) &= -\frac{\gamma_0}{\gamma_0 + \mu_c} \approx -0.968.
\end{align*}
In practice: $\mathcal{R}_0$ is directly proportional to both the amplification level $\alpha$ and the base spread rate $\beta_0$ (unit elasticity), and nearly inversely proportional to the base fatigue rate $\gamma_0$. Holding $I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$ fixed, the critical threshold $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ scales like $(\gamma_0+\mu_c)/\beta_0$: faster fatigue raises the bar for self-sustaining virality, and faster base spread lowers it.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item The equilibrium viral volume $V^*$ is most sensitive to $\gamma_0$ ($S \approx -2.59$), followed by $\alpha$ and $\beta_0$ ($S \approx +1.67$ each). These are all spread-side parameters, which confirms that the spread calibration is the most important empirical anchor.
    \item The pressure parameters $\phi$ and $\psi$ have essentially no effect on $V^*$ but have unit-elastic effects on $\tau^*$ ($S \approx \pm 1.00$). This makes sense: at equilibrium, $\tau^* = \phi V^*/\psi$, so $\tau^*$ is directly proportional to $\phi$ and inversely proportional to $\psi$, while $V^*$ is determined primarily by the spread-side balance.

\end{enumerate}

In this regime these act as two approximately independent levers for platform intervention; the coupling through $\kappa$ and $w$ means the separation is not exact, and it may not hold away from the calibrated parameter range.

\section{Data and calibration}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{The Higgs Twitter dataset}

Our primary dataset is the SNAP Higgs Twitter activity stream \cite{snap, domenico2013}. This dataset records social interactions on Twitter during the period surrounding the announcement of the Higgs boson discovery in July 2012. Each row in the activity file is a tuple of the form
\[
\texttt{userA}\quad \texttt{userB}\quad \texttt{timestamp}\quad \texttt{interaction},
\]
where the interaction type is one of RT (retweet), RE (reply), or MT (mention). The dataset contains no tweet text, only interaction metadata.

The activity counts in the dataset are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Total events: $563{,}069$
    \item Retweets (RT): $354{,}930$
    \item Replies (RE): $36{,}902$
    \item Mentions (MT): $171{,}237$
\end{itemize}

The retweet activity shows a single dominant spike at approximately hour~78, with a peak of $30{,}602$ retweets in that hour. This corresponds to the period immediately following the major announcements at CERN. We use a 100-hour calibration window from hour~68 to hour~167, centered around this main spike, for fitting the spread model. The window is long enough to capture both the spike and the subsequent decay tail.

We use the Higgs dataset in two distinct ways:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Spread calibration.} Retweet counts are used to calibrate the reduced IVF helper model (Section~\ref{sec:helper}).
    \item \textbf{Pressure proxy.} Reply counts are used as a same-dataset proxy for the latent discussion pressure $\tau$ (Section~\ref{sec:proxy}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Reduced IVF helper model}
\label{sec:helper}

We calibrate the spread block using an auxiliary three-compartment IVF helper model derived from the $\dot{I}$, $\dot{V}$, $\dot{F}$ block of IVFS. Relative to the full IVFS model, the helper sets $\tau = 0$ (no pressure feedback), adds a decaying exogenous seed term,
\[
\lambda(t) = \lambda_0\, e^{-d_\lambda t}.
\]
and sets $\delta = 0$ (no moderation attrition). The seed term accounts for the external injection of content into the Higgs cascade (e.g., from news coverage) and improves the fit of the decay tail. The full model's DFE and equilibrium calculations restore the correct $(\delta + \mu_c)$ attrition. The helper model has five free parameters: $\beta_0$, $\gamma_0$, $\lambda_0$, $d_\lambda$, and the initial viral volume $V_0$. The initial condition is normalised so that $I(0) + V(0) = 1$ with $F(0) = 0$; because the output $V(t)$ is subsequently normalised by its peak value, this choice does not affect the fitted parameters.

The optimization uses a bounded Powell search with warm starts, minimizing a weighted sum of squared errors that emphasizes the spike region. The fitted parameter values are:
\begin{align*}
\beta_0 &= 1.000073, & \gamma_0 &= 0.300283, \\
\lambda_0 &= 0.199996, & d_\lambda &= 0.044641.
\end{align*}

\subsection{Calibration diagnostics}

The spread calibration diagnostics from the current implementation are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Weighted loss: $0.002286$
    \item Full-window $R^2$: $0.9247$
    \item Spike-window $R^2$: $0.8891$
    \item Tail mean bias: $+0.0043$
\end{itemize}

The full-window $R^2 = 0.92$ and spike-window $R^2 = 0.89$ indicate that the reduced IVF helper model tracks the normalized retweet series closely on the chosen calibration window. We interpret these as descriptive fit diagnostics rather than as mechanistic validation in themselves.

\subsection{Uncertainty quantification}

We assess parameter uncertainty using two complementary methods.

\paragraph{Parametric bootstrap.} We generate $B = 500$ bootstrap replicates by resampling the Higgs retweet counts from a Poisson distribution with mean equal to the model-fitted count at each time point (parametric bootstrap). Because the hourly cascade counts are temporally dependent, we use this as a rough uncertainty diagnostic rather than as a fully rigorous inferential procedure. Each replicate is refitted, producing a distribution of parameter estimates. The resulting 95\% confidence intervals are:
\begin{align*}
\beta_0 &\in [0.9982,\; 1.0019], \\
\gamma_0 &\in [0.2964,\; 0.3040], \\
\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} &\in [0.3129,\; 0.3199].
\end{align*}
Under this resampling scheme, the narrow intervals support practical identifiability of the spread parameters from the Higgs data.

\paragraph{Profile likelihood.} We compute local profile-likelihood curves by fixing one parameter at a grid of values near its optimum and re-optimizing the remaining parameters. Both $\beta_0$ and $\gamma_0$ show clear, well-defined minima (Figure~\ref{fig:profile}), confirming practical identifiability.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{assets/profile_likelihood.png}
\caption{Profile likelihood curves for the fitted spread parameters $\beta_0$ and $\gamma_0$ on the Higgs calibration window. Each panel fixes one parameter over a grid and re-optimizes the remaining parameters, so the minimum indicates how sharply the data select the fitted value. The clear local minima support practical identifiability of the spread block, which matters because the threshold calculation depends directly on these two parameters.}

\subsection{Benchmark against a classical SIR model}

To compare spread-side fit quality, we benchmark the reduced IVF helper model against a classical three-parameter SIR model fitted to the same normalized retweet window. On the spike-focused comparison window (Figure~\ref{fig:benchmark}), the IVF fit achieves $R^2 \approx 0.889$ while the SIR alternative achieves $R^2 \approx 0.690$. The IVF model captures the asymmetric spike shape and the gradual decay tail significantly better than SIR on this window, which supports the added lifecycle structure on the spread side, especially the distinction between viral and fatigued content.

Just as important is what the benchmark cannot test. A classical SIR model has no state corresponding to latent discussion pressure and no user-retention block, so it cannot address the main policy question of this report. It can only ask whether a three-state epidemic curve can mimic the Higgs spike. The benchmark therefore supports only the spread-side shape advantage of IVF over SIR on this event window; it does not by itself validate the pressure-retention block.

\subsection{Observed proxy for discussion pressure}
\label{sec:proxy}

The interpretation of $\tau$ is one of the most important modelling decisions in this project. In this report, $\tau(t)$ is a latent reply-driven discussion-pressure state generated by viral content and fed back into spread and user loss. The Higgs dataset contains no tweet text, so same-dataset text toxicity cannot be measured directly. Instead, we use same-dataset reply activity (RE) as the primary observable proxy for this pressure state.

Replies on social media are more likely to represent active discussion (including heated or contentious exchanges) than retweets (which are primarily a spreading mechanism) or mentions (which may be purely informational). High reply volume during a viral event is therefore a plausible indicator of elevated discussion intensity or contentious engagement, even without text-level toxicity labels.

We construct a proxy family and select the best-supported target:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Smoothed RE proxy: $R^2 \approx 0.7422$ (unconstrained fit).
    \item Reply-rate proxy $\mathrm{RE}/(\mathrm{RT}+1)$: $R^2 \approx -1.14$ (poor fit).
    \item Composite proxy: $R^2 \approx -0.53$ (poor fit).
\end{itemize}
The RE proxy is the clear winner in terms of fit quality for the chosen one-timescale pressure dynamics, and it is also the simplest proxy to interpret behaviourally.

The figure matters because it shows both the fit of the RE proxy to the chosen pressure dynamics and the remaining identification gap in the pressure block.}

\subsection{External Jigsaw text toxicity reference}

The Jigsaw toxicity dataset \cite{borkan2019}, when supplied separately by the user in \texttt{data/toxicity/train.csv}, provides an external text-toxicity reference for the magnitude of $\tau$. It is not part of the core Higgs dataset and is not required for the main spread calibration. This dataset contains text-level toxicity labels from a different platform context, namely Wikipedia talk pages. We use it only as an external scale reference for $\tau$, not to fit the pressure dynamics and not to validate the Higgs reply-to-pressure mapping. The mean Jigsaw toxicity rate is approximately $0.1017$, which gives an external scale comparison value of $\phi \approx 0.1459$ when $\psi = 0.1$ and the moderate scenario equilibrium viral volume is used for scaling.

\subsection{Reference-constrained calibration}

The pressure parameters $(\phi, \psi)$ are not uniquely identified from the same-dataset proxy alone. The unconstrained fit achieves good proxy agreement ($R^2 \approx 0.74$) but at a pressure scale ($\tau^*_{\mathrm{mod}} \approx 0.067$) that is inconsistent with the external Jigsaw scale reference. We resolve this identification problem partially through a \emph{reference-constrained} approach.

The external Jigsaw text toxicity rate anchors the steady state gain ratio:
\[
r = \frac{\phi}{\psi} = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{ref}}}{V^*_{\mathrm{mod}}} \approx \frac{0.1017}{0.0697} \approx 1.46.
\]
This constrains $(\phi, \psi)$ to the line $\phi = r\psi$ in parameter space. We then treat $\psi$ as the single free parameter on this constraint line and choose the value that best matches the same-dataset RE proxy dynamics. This one-dimensional optimization gives:
\[
\phi_{\mathrm{rc}} \approx 0.417, \qquad \psi_{\mathrm{rc}} \approx 0.286,
\]
with proxy-fit $R^2 \approx 0.41$.

The reference-constrained configuration achieves better proxy-fit quality ($R^2 \approx 0.41$) than either the baseline ($R^2 \approx 0.18$) or the plain external-scale setting ($R^2 \approx 0.31$), while maintaining the externally anchored pressure scale. The remaining gap between the constrained ($R^2 \approx 0.41$) and boundary-constrained ($R^2 \approx 0.74$) fits is the substantive identification limit of the one-timescale pressure block. Closing this gap would require either a dataset with both spread data and directly observed text-toxicity labels, or a multi-timescale $\tau$ model.

The empirical picture the calibration establishes is: the spread side is tightly identified (bootstrap 95\% CI on $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ spans $[0.313,\,0.320]$), but the pressure block is constrained only along the ratio $\phi/\psi \approx 1.46$. The individual values of $\phi$ and $\psi$ require an independent text-toxicity measurement, or an independent same-platform pressure measurement, to pin down separately.

We also checked whether the spread-side conclusions are sensitive to the exact calibration window and time discretization. Re-fitting the reduced IVF model on 80 and 100 hour windows with the original 1 hour discretization still gives strong fits ($R^2 \approx 0.91$ and $0.92$), while the 120 hour window is noticeably weaker because it pulls in a much longer low-volume tail. A harsher stress test with 2 hour binning performs much worse again. The model was formulated and calibrated at the 1 hour event scale around the dominant spike, so both long-tail expansion and coarse aggregation weaken fit quality. This matters because it shows the main spread conclusion is robust near the calibrated spike window, while also clarifying where that calibration stops being reliable.

\section{Numerical results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Policy scenarios}

We compare three amplification scenarios that represent different platform policies:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Engagement-first} ($\alpha = 0.9$): aggressive amplification, representing a platform that prioritizes short-run engagement metrics.
    \item \textbf{Moderate} ($\alpha = 0.5$): balanced amplification, representing a platform that partially accounts for content quality alongside engagement.
    \item \textbf{Health-first} ($\alpha = 0.2$): conservative amplification, representing a platform that prioritizes user well-being over engagement metrics.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:scenarios} reports the long-run equilibrium values under each scenario with the baseline pressure parameters.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Long-run equilibrium values under the three policy scenarios (baseline pressure block).}
\label{tab:scenarios}
\begin{tabular}{lccccl}
\toprule
Scenario & $\mathcal{R}_0$ & $V^*$ & $\tau^*$ & $U^*$ & Regime \\
\midrule
Engagement-first ($\alpha = 0.9$) & $2.84$ & $0.1203$ & $0.0673$ & $29.88$ & Above threshold \\
Moderate ($\alpha = 0.5$) & $1.58$ & $0.0697$ & $0.0390$ & $35.97$ & Above threshold \\
Health-first ($\alpha = 0.2$) & $0.63$ & $0.0000$ & $0.0000$ & $50.00$ & Below threshold \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The results show a clear pattern:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{The health-first scenario eliminates sustained virality.} With $\mathcal{R}_0 = 0.63 < 1$, viral content cannot sustain itself, and the system converges to the DFE with $V^* = \tau^* = 0$ and $U^* = \nu/\lambda_u = 50$. This is the maximum possible user retention.

    \item \textbf{Higher amplification increases viral volume and pressure.} Moving from moderate to engagement-first increases $V^*$ by 73\% (from $0.070$ to $0.120$) and $\tau^*$ by 73\% (from $0.039$ to $0.067$). The proportional increase is the same because $\tau^* = \phi V^*/\psi$ is linear in $V^*$ at equilibrium.

    \item \textbf{Higher amplification reduces user retention.} The engagement-first scenario retains only $29.9$ users compared to $36.0$ in the moderate scenario and $50.0$ in the health-first scenario. This is a 40\% reduction relative to the health-first baseline ($U^* = 50$).

    \item \textbf{The tradeoff is real but nonlinear.} The engagement-first scenario does produce more short-run viral content, but the long-run user base is substantially smaller. Whether this tradeoff is favourable depends on what the platform optimizes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Continuation in amplification}

Figure~\ref{fig:results} shows the results of a continuation sweep over $\alpha \in [0.05, 1.0]$. The sweep reveals:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item A clear threshold crossing at $\alpha \approx 0.3165$. Below this point, $V^* = \tau^* = 0$.
    \item Above the threshold, both $V^*$ and $\tau^*$ increase monotonically with $\alpha$.
    \item $U^*$ decreases monotonically with $\alpha$ above the threshold, reflecting pressure-dependent attrition.
    \item The constructed composite engagement index $E^* = \alpha V^* U^*$ increases with $\alpha$ in the tested range. We use this only as a scenario score that combines amplification, viral volume, and user base, not as a directly observed engagement measurement from the data.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pressure sensitivity}

We perform a local sensitivity sweep over the pressure forcing parameter $\phi$ with $\psi$ fixed at its baseline value of $0.1$. The purpose is to check whether the qualitative policy ranking (engagement-first $>$ moderate $>$ health-first in terms of pressure) is robust to changes in $\phi$.

Figure~\ref{fig:phisens} shows the results. Across the entire tested range of $\phi$, the ordering of the three scenarios is preserved: the engagement-first scenario always produces the highest long-run pressure, the moderate scenario remains in the middle, and the health-first scenario stays at zero pressure because it remains below threshold. The baseline value $\phi = 0.056$ and the external-reference value $\phi \approx 0.146$ are both within the range where the qualitative conclusions hold.

\subsection{Multi-window validation}

Note that these windows overlap substantially (e.g., windows~2 and~3 share hours 120 to 151, a 32-hour overlap), so the refits are not independent; the purpose is to test whether the model shape fits different phases of the cascade, not to provide independent replication.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Identification limitations.}
The pressure parameters $(\phi, \psi)$ are not jointly identified from the available data. The spread block is well-identified (bootstrap CIs are narrow, profile likelihood shows clear minima), but the pressure block relies on a reply proxy that does not separately pin down the forcing and relaxation timescales. The reference-constrained calibration resolves this partially: it anchors $\phi/\psi$ to the Jigsaw external reference but cannot determine the individual values. The boundary-constrained fit (Table~\ref{tab:tauconfigs}) reaches the upper bound $\psi = 1.0$ imposed during optimization; the true optimum may lie at $\psi > 1$, and relaxing this bound is a natural next step once independent pressure measurements are available.

\paragraph{Data limitations.}
The spread model is fitted on a single event cascade (Higgs boson announcement, July 2012). The multi-window validation (Section~\ref{sec:windowval}) shows that the IVF burst-and-decay shape continues to fit several active phases of this event, but the parameter values are specific to this cascade and this platform. Calibration on independent events from different topics, time periods, and platforms is needed to assess whether the threshold value and spread rates carry over.

\paragraph{Modelling limitations.}
Three structural simplifications bound the model's scope. First, homogeneous mixing: all content items in a compartment interact identically, ignoring network topology, community structure, and content heterogeneity. Second, deterministic dynamics: the model cannot capture stochastic fluctuations or rare-event behaviour inherent in individual sharing decisions. Third, scalar pressure: the latent state $\tau$ collapses the multidimensional nature of online toxicity (hate speech, harassment, misinformation) into a single index; a richer model might track multiple pressure types separately.

\paragraph{External validity limitations.}
The rage-bait operational definition (high $\tau/V$ ratio for $V>0$, Section~\ref{sec:ragebait-def}) remains a model-internal structural indicator rather than a validated external label. Future work should test whether this structural signature coincides with content independently labelled as inflammatory (e.g.\ using Jigsaw or Ruddit labels). Similarly, the DFE-based $\mathcal{R}_0$ derivation uses linearization of the $\dot{V}$ equation; a full next-generation matrix treatment would confirm the result and clarify the roles of individual model components.

Despite these limitations, the calibrated model supports the existence of a sharp amplification threshold and a monotone amplification-retention tradeoff. What it cannot yet establish is whether the specific threshold value $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$ or the magnitude of the retention loss carry over across platforms, content types, or event scales.

\section{Questions from the presentation}
\label{sec:questions}

This section records questions raised during the class presentation and provides answers grounded in the model, calibration, and analysis developed in this report. All questions were answered during the presentation by Neel Sarkar.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q1.} \textit{Given the importance of $\alpha$ (the platform amplification strength), how do you propose determining this parameter methodologically from input data? What characteristics of the social media platform, dominant user pool, or the content itself do you think are most relevant?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A1.}
In our framework $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is a \emph{scenario} parameter, not a calibrated one: we study the system across the full amplification range rather than pinning down a single empirical value (Table~\ref{tab:params}).
Determining $\alpha$ from data would require access to internal platform recommendation metrics, specifically the fraction of content impressions attributable to algorithmic promotion versus organic (chronological or follower-based) reach.
The most relevant platform characteristics are the engagement signals the ranking algorithm optimises (clicks, reshares, dwell time), the ratio of algorithmically recommended impressions to chronologically sorted impressions, and any editorial or policy constraints the platform places on amplification.
User-pool characteristics such as average session length and propensity to reshare would also affect how strongly a given $\alpha$ translates into viral conversion, since these factors are folded into the base spread rate $\beta_0$ in our model.
In practice, $\alpha$ is best understood as a platform-level control knob: our contribution is not to estimate it but to show that the system's qualitative behaviour, including the threshold at $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$, changes sharply as $\alpha$ varies.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q2.} \textit{What are the critical assumptions of the compartmental / epidemiological model used in your work that may prevent the practical use or realism of your model?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A2.}
The assumptions are listed explicitly in Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}. The ones most likely to limit practical realism are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Homogeneous mixing.} All content items in a compartment are treated identically, ignoring network topology and community structure. Real platforms have clustered communities where content spreads locally before crossing network boundaries.
  \item \textbf{Deterministic dynamics.} The model tracks mean trajectories and cannot capture stochastic fluctuations, rare super-spreading events, or the randomness inherent in individual sharing decisions.
  \item \textbf{Scalar pressure.} All forms of harmful discussion (hate speech, harassment, heated debate) are collapsed into a single latent variable $\tau$. A more realistic model might distinguish multiple pressure types with different feedback strengths.
  \item \textbf{Aggregate user pool.} The single variable $U$ does not resolve user heterogeneity, meaning the model cannot distinguish between highly engaged power users and casual browsers who may respond very differently to toxicity.
  \item \textbf{Single-cascade calibration.} The spread parameters are fitted on one event from one platform. Generalisation to other topics, time periods, or platforms is untested.
\end{enumerate}
These assumptions are deliberate tradeoffs between granularity and identifiability (Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}): they keep the system tractable and calibratable with publicly available aggregate data, at the cost of resolution on micro-level effects.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q3.} \textit{How do you distinguish between different web content types: ignored, virally reshared, and suppressed?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A3.}
The compartments $I$, $V$, $F$, $S$ are lifecycle stages, not content-topic categories.
Content enters the system in the ignored state $I$ (posted but not yet amplified).
The transition $I \to V$ occurs through the mass-action term $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\,I\,V$: ignored content becomes viral when it interacts with already-viral content in the presence of algorithmic amplification (the factor $\alpha$ in $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}$).
Viral content fatigues ($V \to F$) at rate $\gamma_{\mathrm{eff}}\,V$ as engagement momentum decays.
Content is suppressed ($I \to S$) by platform moderation at rate $\delta\,I$.
In the empirical calibration, the viral compartment $V$ is proxied by normalised hourly retweet counts from the Higgs dataset, and the discussion-pressure variable $\tau$ is proxied by reply counts.
The model does not classify individual posts by topic; it classifies the aggregate content mass by its position in the visibility lifecycle.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q4.} \textit{What limitations do you think your model has?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A4.}
The full discussion is in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}. In summary:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Identification.} The spread block is well-identified (bootstrap CI on $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ spans $[0.313,\,0.320]$), but the pressure parameters $(\phi,\psi)$ are only partially identified: the ratio $\phi/\psi$ is anchored by the external Jigsaw reference, while the individual values cannot be separately pinned down without independent text-toxicity data.
  \item \textbf{Data.} The model is calibrated on a single 100-hour event cascade from Twitter in 2012. Multi-cascade, multi-platform validation is needed.
  \item \textbf{Structural.} Homogeneous mixing, deterministic flows, scalar pressure, and an aggregate user pool are all simplifications. Each trades micro-level realism for macro-level tractability.
  \item \textbf{External validity.} The operational definition of rage-bait (elevated $\tau/V$ ratio) has not been validated against ground-truth text-toxicity annotations on the same dataset.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q5.} \textit{Is there any way to capture the nature of different social media platforms (e.g., responses to ragebait, tolerance to toxicity)?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A5.}
Yes, the model already encodes platform-specific behaviour through several parameters.
The amplification parameter $\alpha$ captures how aggressively the platform's algorithm promotes engaging content: a platform that prioritises engagement would have high $\alpha$, while one that limits algorithmic amplification would have low $\alpha$.
The moderation rate $\delta$ captures how quickly the platform removes or de-ranks content.
Tolerance to toxicity is captured by $w$ (the weight of pressure on user attrition): a platform whose users are more tolerant of heated discussion would have a lower $w$, meaning a given level of $\tau$ causes less user departure.
Different platforms would therefore correspond to different $(\alpha, \delta, w)$ triples.
However, the current calibration is specific to Twitter during the Higgs event, so the parameter values themselves would need to be re-estimated for each platform. Cross-platform calibration is a natural direction for future work (Section~\ref{sec:nextsteps}).

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q6.} \textit{In the rage-bait scenario, based on social media recommendation systems, content would spread to a target population and then reduce recommendation until the target audience all received the message\,. So target population should also be included as a factor in the model?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A6.}
The model captures a finite-audience effect implicitly through two mechanisms.
First, the mass-action conversion term $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\,I\,V$ depends on the product of ignored and viral content: as content goes viral and the ignored pool $I$ is depleted, the rate of new viral conversion slows, which is analogous to susceptible depletion in an SIR model.
Second, the user pool $U$ shrinks under toxicity pressure (Eq.~\ref{eq:U}), which reduces content creation via the saturating term $\rho U/(1+U)$, further limiting the available audience.
What the model does \emph{not} have is an explicit ``reached audience'' compartment that would track how many users have already seen a particular piece of content.
Adding such a compartment (for example, splitting $U$ into ``not yet exposed'' and ``already exposed'' sub-pools) would allow direct modelling of the recommendation saturation effect described in the question. This is a plausible extension, though it would add parameters that require per-user exposure data to identify.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q7.} \textit{Is the assumption of average users implicitly? Will people from different regions or groups behave differently?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A7.}
Yes, the model assumes a single homogeneous user pool $U$ with uniform attrition sensitivity (Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}, Assumption~5).
In reality, users from different regions, age groups, or interest communities differ in their exposure to content, their engagement patterns, and their sensitivity to toxicity.
Incorporating this heterogeneity would require stratifying $U$ into multiple sub-pools $U_1, U_2, \ldots$ with group-specific attrition rates $\lambda_{u,i}$ and toxicity sensitivities $w_i$, or adopting a network-aware formulation with degree-dependent transmission rates.
Each additional user group adds parameters that need empirical grounding, which the aggregate-level Higgs dataset cannot provide.
The aggregate approach is therefore a deliberate choice matched to the resolution of the available data: it captures the \emph{directional} retention cost of amplification (more pressure $\Rightarrow$ fewer users) without claiming to resolve which sub-populations are most affected.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q8.} \textit{Could introducing stochastic shocks or time-varying parameters better capture sudden viral bursts or platform interventions?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A8.}
Yes, and both are listed as future directions (Section~\ref{sec:nextsteps}).
A stochastic differential equation (SDE) version of the model, adding Wiener-process noise to the $\dot{V}$ and $\dot{\tau}$ equations, would capture random fluctuations and rare super-spreading events that the deterministic model smooths over.
Time-varying parameters would allow modelling of platform interventions that change the amplification policy mid-cascade: for example, $\alpha(t)$ could step down at a known intervention time, and the model would predict how the system relaxes to a new equilibrium.
The current deterministic, constant-parameter model is appropriate for studying the \emph{mean} cascade shape and the equilibrium structure, which is what the threshold and policy-comparison results require.
Adding stochasticity or time dependence would increase realism but also increase the number of parameters that need to be identified, and the analytical results (DFE stability, threshold formula, sensitivity indices) would need to be replaced by their stochastic or non-autonomous counterparts.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q9.} \textit{You seem to define toxicity as the quantity of comments on a post: how do you measure positive or constructive conversation?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A9.}
The model does not distinguish between constructive and toxic conversation.
The latent variable $\tau$ represents aggregate reply-driven discussion pressure, proxied empirically by hourly reply volume from the Higgs dataset (Section~\ref{sec:proxy}).
High reply volume is interpreted as elevated discussion pressure regardless of whether the replies are constructive, neutral, or hostile.
This is a known limitation: the Higgs dataset contains only interaction metadata (timestamps and event types), not tweet text, so per-message sentiment or toxicity classification is not possible within the same dataset.
The external Jigsaw text-toxicity reference \cite{borkan2019} is used only to anchor the \emph{scale} of $\tau$, not to classify individual messages.
Separating constructive from toxic conversation within the model would require either a dataset that includes both interaction metadata and text-level toxicity labels, or a multi-dimensional pressure state that tracks positive and negative engagement separately. This is a natural extension but is beyond what the current data can support.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q10.} \textit{How did you calculate that $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$? What would it mean for there to be a bifurcation in $\alpha$?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A10.}
The critical value is computed analytically.
We linearise the $\dot{V}$ equation around the disease-free equilibrium (DFE) and define the basic reproduction number $\mathcal{R}_0 = \alpha\,\beta_0\,I_{\mathrm{DFE}} / (\gamma_0 + \mu_c)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:R0}).
Setting $\mathcal{R}_0 = 1$ and solving for $\alpha$ gives
\[
\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} = \frac{\gamma_0 + \mu_c}{\beta_0 \cdot I_{\mathrm{DFE}}}.
\]
With calibrated values $\beta_0 \approx 1.0001$, $\gamma_0 \approx 0.3003$, $\mu_c = 0.01$, and $I_{\mathrm{DFE}} \approx 0.9804$, this evaluates to $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:alpha_crit}).

For the calibrated parameter set studied in this report, the local bifurcation at $\alpha=\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ is forward transcritical (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, Proposition~\ref{prop:forward}).
That means that, near the threshold, a positive equilibrium branch emerges continuously from the DFE as $\alpha$ crosses $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ from below, and this branch is locally stable near the bifurcation point while the DFE becomes unstable.
Away from the threshold, the report relies on the separate calibrated existence-and-uniqueness argument (Proposition~\ref{prop:unique}) together with numerical Jacobian checks at representative scenarios.
In practical terms, the bifurcation means the platform ``switches on'' a self-sustaining cycle of viral spread and discussion pressure once amplification exceeds the critical level.
The transition is sharp: Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation} shows the onset of $V^*$ at the threshold.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q11.} \textit{How would you recommend moderation be enforced to reduce rage bait and toxicity? How do non-toxic factors like bots affect the critical threshold?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A11.}
The model suggests two complementary moderation strategies.
First, \emph{reduce $\alpha$ below $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$}: if the platform dials amplification below the critical level, the DFE is stable and sustained virality collapses entirely. This is the cleanest intervention because it eliminates the feedback loop at its source.
Second, \emph{increase the moderation rate $\delta$}: diverting more content from $I$ to $S$ before it goes viral lowers the DFE value of $I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:dfe}), which raises $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$. In other words, more aggressive content moderation makes it harder for the self-sustaining viral cycle to start.

Bots would affect the model primarily by inflating the effective content creation rate (increasing $\rho$) and potentially amplifying the $I \to V$ transition by generating artificial engagement.
A higher $\rho$ increases $I_{\mathrm{DFE}}$ (more ignored content available for viral conversion), which \emph{lowers} $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ and makes self-sustaining virality easier to trigger.
Bot mitigation could therefore be modelled as reducing $\rho$ (less spam content entering the system) or increasing $\delta$ (faster removal of bot-generated content), both of which raise $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ and make the platform safer.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q12.} \textit{Does a user's algorithm affect how prone they are to seeing certain types of content? For example, one user might see more extreme content than another, how does your model account for personalization algorithms that change audience exposure?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A12.}
The model does not account for personalization.
The amplification parameter $\alpha$ represents an \emph{average platform-level} amplification strength, not a per-user quantity.
Under homogeneous mixing (Assumption~3, Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}), all content items in a compartment interact with the full user pool identically.
In reality, personalization algorithms create heterogeneous exposure: some users are shown more extreme or engaging content than others, depending on their interaction history.
Capturing this would require either a user-stratified model (multiple user pools with different effective $\alpha_i$ values) or a network-aware formulation where each node has its own exposure distribution.
This is an important limitation: personalization can concentrate toxicity exposure on a subset of users, amplifying the retention cost for that subset while leaving others unaffected. The aggregate model detects the average directional effect (more amplification $\Rightarrow$ lower retention) but cannot resolve which users bear the cost.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q13.} \textit{The definitions of ``toxicity'' and ``rage-baiting'' can be subjective, how does your model account for this? Who determines what content is toxic or rage bait?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A13.}
The model avoids subjective content labelling by using an \emph{operational} definition of rage-bait (Section~\ref{sec:ragebait-def}).
Content is rage-bait whenever the ratio of discussion pressure to viral volume ($\tau/V$) is elevated, for states with $V>0$.
This is a structural, model-internal definition based on observable interaction patterns: high reply activity relative to passive resharing, not a human editorial judgement or a validated external content label.
The latent variable $\tau$ is not a text-toxicity classifier score; it is a behavioural aggregate inferred from reply volume.
No individual or committee determines what is ``toxic'' within the model. The model instead asks: does the \emph{interaction pattern} look like high-arousal discussion pressure?
The Jigsaw dataset \cite{borkan2019}, which does use human-annotated text-level toxicity labels, is used only as an external scale reference to calibrate the magnitude of $\tau$, not to classify any content in the Higgs dataset.
The subjectivity concern is valid in general, but in our framework it is reframed operationally through behavioural proxies rather than direct content labels.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Q14.} \textit{Could this model be applied to other types of content that produce less intense emotional responses?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{A14.}
Yes.
The IVFS compartmental structure (ignored $\to$ viral $\to$ fatigued $\to$ suppressed) describes a \emph{content lifecycle} that is not specific to rage-bait or high-arousal content.
Any content type that exhibits burst-and-decay dynamics, including news, entertainment, scientific announcements, or memes, could in principle be modelled with the same framework.
For less emotionally intense content, the pressure parameters would change: lower $\phi$ (less viral-to-pressure forcing), lower $w$ (weaker effect of pressure on user departure), and possibly higher $\psi$ (faster pressure relaxation).
The threshold structure ($\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$) and the amplification--retention tradeoff could plausibly still apply, but the quantitative values would differ.
The key requirement is a dataset with observable spread activity (retweets or shares) and engagement activity (replies or comments) for calibration.
The Higgs boson cascade used in this report is itself an example of scientific content rather than politically provocative content, so it at least shows that the framework can be applied outside a purely political case study.
What the current report supports is a plausible extension of the framework to other high-discussion content types, not a general empirical validation across content domains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of results}

We have developed and analyzed a deterministic compartmental ODE model for content lifecycle dynamics on social media. The model tracks six state variables: four content lifecycle compartments (ignored, viral, fatigued, suppressed), a latent discussion-pressure state, and an active-user pool. The key results are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Well-posedness.} The model preserves nonnegativity and admits a bounded invariant region (Propositions~\ref{prop:nonneg} and \ref{prop:bounded}).

    \item \textbf{Clear amplification threshold.} The disease-free equilibrium is locally asymptotically stable if and only if $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:dfe}). For the calibrated parameter set, when $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$ a unique positive equilibrium exists (Proposition~\ref{prop:unique}), and the bifurcation at $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$ is proven to be forward locally near the threshold (Proposition~\ref{prop:forward}).

    \item \textbf{Strong spread calibration.} The reduced IVF helper model fits the Higgs retweet data with full-window $R^2 = 0.92$, and the spread parameters are tightly identified (bootstrap CI width $< 0.01$). The IVF model outperforms a classical SIR benchmark on the spike window (spike-window $R^2 = 0.89$ vs.\ $0.69$).

    \item \textbf{Robust policy ranking.} Across all tested pressure configurations, the qualitative ordering is preserved: higher amplification increases viral volume and discussion pressure while lowering user retention.

    \item \textbf{Pressure identification.} The pressure block is partially identified through a combination of same-dataset reply proxy and an external text-toxicity scale reference. The reference-constrained calibration provides the best available estimate ($R^2 \approx 0.41$), but a gap remains relative to the boundary-constrained optimum ($R^2 \approx 0.74$, where the optimizer reached the upper bound on $\psi$).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Significance}

Within the calibrated regime studied here, a sharp amplification threshold ($\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.3165$) governs whether virality and discussion pressure self-sustain. Below it, both collapse to zero and the platform retains its full user base. Above it, the system locks into a feedback cycle whose equilibrium viral volume, pressure level, and user attrition all increase monotonically with how aggressively the platform amplifies.

The distinctive feature of this result is that it follows from a \emph{content centric} model. Prior compartmental work on social media dynamics almost uniformly classifies users into behavioural states, susceptible, infected, recovered, and variants thereof. This requires knowledge of individual user exposure histories that are rarely available in public data, and it places the policy variable (amplification) as an implicit modifier of user-to-user transmission rates. By instead classifying content items into lifecycle stages, the IVFS state variables correspond directly to quantities platforms observe and control: content volume in each visibility tier, reply rate as a discussion-pressure signal, and active user count. The amplification parameter $\alpha$ is a platform control variable, not a latent individual trait.

This alignment between model state and platform observables makes the threshold analysis more directly expressible in content-level observables. Determining whether $\alpha$ clears $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$ uses content-level event counts rather than inferred user-state data. In this calibrated case study, the six-state ODE produces a quantitative threshold and a policy scenario comparison with interpretable equilibrium values, though broader actionability still depends on stronger cross-event validation and better identification of the pressure block.

\subsection{Next steps}
\label{sec:nextsteps}

Some directions for future work:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Next-generation matrix for $\mathcal{R}_0$.} The current threshold is derived from DFE linearization of the $\dot{V}$ equation. A full next-generation matrix derivation would provide a more rigorous foundation and clarify the roles of the different model components in the reproduction number.

    \item \textbf{Global stability.} We have established local stability of both the DFE (Proposition~\ref{prop:dfe}) and the positive equilibrium (numerically). Global stability results (for example, using Lyapunov functions) would strengthen the analytical foundation.

    \item \textbf{Hopf bifurcation.} Proposition~\ref{prop:forward} establishes a forward transcritical bifurcation for the calibrated parameter set near $\alpha_{\mathrm{crit}}$. We have not, however, ruled out Hopf bifurcations at extreme parameter values, which would produce oscillatory dynamics rather than convergence to a steady state.

    \item \textbf{Multi-cascade validation.} Calibrating the model on multiple independent event cascades from different platforms and topics would test the generalizability of the content-centric framework.

    \item \textbf{Multi-timescale pressure.} Replacing the one-timescale pressure block with a two-timescale or state-dependent model could resolve the identification gap between the constrained and unconstrained pressure fits.

    \item \textbf{Stochastic extension.} A stochastic differential equation version of the model would allow us to study the effects of noise on threshold behaviour and equilibrium fluctuations.

    \item \textbf{Network-aware extension.} Incorporating network structure (e.g., through degree-dependent transmission rates) would relax the homogeneous mixing assumption and allow the model to capture community-level dynamics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility notes}
\label{app:repro}

All code used in this project is available in the repository. The main scripts are:
\begin{verbatim}
python -m code.ivfs_validation
python -m code.equilibrium_analysis
python -m code.benchmark_models
python -m code.toxicity_calibration
\end{verbatim}
The main outputs (figures, diagnostics, and run metadata) are written to the \texttt{assets/} directory. The Python environment is specified in \texttt{requirements.txt}. The Higgs dataset is expected locally, with optional external toxicity files supplied separately by the user. The code does not download datasets from the internet.

\section{Identification note for the pressure block}
\label{app:ident}

The empirical package identifies different parts of the model with different strengths:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Higgs retweet counts} strongly identify the spread-side parameters $(\beta_0, \gamma_0, \lambda_0, d_\lambda, V_0)$ on the main 100-hour window. Bootstrap CIs are narrow and profile likelihood curves show clear minima.

    \item \textbf{Higgs reply activity} partially identifies the pressure \emph{shape}: it distinguishes better and worse relaxation timescales $\psi$, but only through a proxy, and therefore without fixing an absolute pressure scale.

    \item \textbf{The Jigsaw reference} identifies the steady-state gain ratio $r = \phi/\psi = \tau_{\mathrm{ref}} / V^*_{\mathrm{mod}}$, constraining $(\phi, \psi)$ to a line rather than to a point.

    \item \textbf{The reference-constrained fit} combines items two and three: it restricts $(\phi, \psi)$ to the externally anchored line $\phi = r\psi$ and then chooses the $\psi$ on that line that best matches the RE proxy. This is the most informative single estimate currently available, but it leaves a residual gap relative to the unconstrained same-dataset optimum.
\end{itemize}

\section{Possible model extensions}
\label{app:extensions}

Natural extensions of the present framework include:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item A full next-generation matrix derivation of $\mathcal{R}_0$.
    \item Theorem-level local and global stability results using Lyapunov methods.
    \item A multi-timescale or state-dependent pressure model.
    \item A latent observation model for $\tau$ with RE and MT as noisy observables.
    \item Independent-event spread validation beyond the Higgs record.
    \item Network-aware formulations with degree-dependent rates.
    \item Stochastic extensions for uncertainty quantification in the dynamics.
\end{enumerate}
